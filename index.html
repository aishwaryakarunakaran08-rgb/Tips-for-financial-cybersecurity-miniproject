Tips for Financial Cybersecurity

Protecting your personal and financial information online is more critical than ever. Always use strong, unique passwords for your banking accounts, enable multi-factor authentication (MFA), and beware of phishing emails or texts asking for your credentials.

Customer Account Takeover

Account takeover occurs when criminals steal customer credentials — usernames, passwords, or MFA codes — to gain unauthorized access to personal and financial accounts. Understanding how these attacks happen is your first line of defence.

How Account Takeovers Happen

Phishing & Smishing

Fake emails or SMS messages impersonating banks or regulators to harvest passwords, account numbers, or Social Security numbers.

Fake Websites

Lookalike login pages mimicking real financial institutions, luring victims via search results or malicious email links.

Social Engineering

Fraudsters call posing as bank representatives to trick you into revealing personal account details over the phone.

Stolen Credentials

Data breaches, dark web sales, keyloggers, or dumpster-diving for discarded statements can expose your login information.

Signs of an Account Takeover

Unfamiliar or unauthorized transactions, money movements, or deposits High risk
Missing funds or securities from your account
Unauthorized updates to account information — address, email, or phone number
Unexpected notifications about account changes you did not request
Large volume of spam texts or emails shortly after logging in Warning
Sudden loss of mobile phone service — a possible SIM-swapping attack High risk
Missing account statements, confirmations, or other financial documents
Unfamiliar accounts or creditors appearing on your credit report
Funds that have been frozen by your financial institution

Cybersecurity Tips to Safeguard Your Account

Follow these 8 essential practices to protect your financial accounts from unauthorized access and cyber threats.

8 Essential Security Practices

1

Watch What You Click

Avoid clicking suspicious links or downloading attachments from unknown sources. Instead, go directly to your financial institution's website or app to verify any communication.

2

Use Strong Passwords

Use a unique, strong password for every account and change them regularly. A password manager app can suggest and securely store individual passwords for each account.

3

Enable Multifactor Authentication

MFA adds a second layer beyond your password — such as a texted code, fingerprint, or facial recognition — significantly reducing the risk of account takeover.

4

Maintain Device Security

Keep security software with antivirus, anti-spam, and spyware detection up to date. Enable automatic updates. If using cloud storage, verify the provider uses MFA and data encryption.

5

Use Your Own Device

Avoid accessing financial accounts on public computers — they may contain software that captures passwords. If you must, clear your cache, temporary files, and browsing history after logging out.

6

Browse Carefully

Use only secure sites starting with "https" and a padlock icon. Avoid multitasking across sites while logged in to prevent session stealing. Always click "Log Out" — don't just close the tab.

7

Be Cyber Safe on Wi-Fi

Public Wi-Fi at airports, hotels, and restaurants often has reduced security. Beware of fake hotspots with familiar-sounding names. Warning signs: slow connections or no terms-of-service prompt.

8

Review All Correspondence

Review account activity and monthly statements thoroughly as soon as they're available. Keep your contact info current and report any unauthorized activity to your financial institution immediately.

Quick Security Checklist

Don't click suspicious links
Use unique passwords
Enable MFA on all accounts
Keep antivirus up to date
Avoid public computers
Check for https + padlock
Avoid public Wi-Fi for banking
Review statements monthly
